The doctoral thesis presented by Paul Guhennec **explores how computational methods can be employed to enrich architectural history, using the case study of the city of Venice**. This research is based on the exploitation of a comprehensive point cloud representation of the city, coordinated with a robust geographic information system (GIS) enriched by various historical and contemporary sources. The 366-page volume submitted for oral examination includes a textual component, a bibliography, indexes, and an atlas. The text, approximately 80,000 words, is richly illustrated with 120 visualizations produced by the author. This visual material is integral to the argument developed by the author.

In the introduction, the candidate rightly emphasizes **the extraordinary opportunity of utilizing a high-definition photogrammetric point cloud representation of Venice**, while also addressing the challenges posed by such massive datasets, particularly in terms of precision and usability. This novel type of source invites reflection on the issues of close reading versus distant reading, aiming to transcend the dichotomy between the “culture of the letter” and the “culture of numbers.” Here, montage in conjunction with visualization is proposed as a means of enabling new readings. Beyond the utilization of this unprecedented material and its methodological implications, the thesis builds on a rich historiography, addressing questions of scale in urban studies and examining the combinatory dimensions of architecture and urban space. Specifically, the research is rooted in a typo-morphological approach, particularly developed for studying the urban fabric of Venice.



---

**Beyond the exceptional corpus assembled for this doctoral research, the ambition to “weave together architectural history, computational thinking, and the city of Venice” must first be commended. We believe that the work successfully achieves this goal.** Not only does it innovate by developing and applying novel computational approaches to architecture and urban space, but it also effectively reconciles the formalism of computational methods with an architectural discussion grounded in historiography. The denaturalizing character of the digital approach offers a fresh perspective on urban analysis and can be juxtaposed with numerous previous studies of Venice. Here, computational tools introduce a shift in the scale of analysis and raise new questions.

**The emphasis on visualization in this work is particularly noteworthy, both in its scope and relevance.** Visualization is not merely illustrative; the computational manipulation of the point cloud and structured data produces genuine visual arguments that directly support the thesis. These diagrammatic images hold significant heuristic and hermeneutic value.



---

**Most importantly, the doctoral thesis demonstrates a strong epistemological orientation.** Each chapter and phase of the research includes efforts at definition and formalization, as well as a reflexive discussion of the sources and results. Beyond offering new insights into the urban fabric of Venice, this thesis contributes significantly by proposing computational methods applicable in other contexts within architectural and urban history.

**At the same time, the discussion never neglects architectural considerations, grounding distant readings in specific observations.** The computational analysis results are always integrated with historical approaches, presenting a clear and engaging argument. While some works in the digital humanities field may be criticized for insufficient disciplinary grounding, this is far from the case here. This is why we believe this thesis could become a landmark, demonstrating the promising contributions of computational architectural analysis.
